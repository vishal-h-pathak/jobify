## How He Communicates

- **Plain and direct.** Short sentences. States the point, then supports it.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Talks about specific systems and outcomes, not
  "leveraging synergies." Names the technology and the number.
- **Calibrates to audience.** Explains a concept from first principles to a
  newcomer, or jumps to the trade-off with a senior peer.
- **Honest about scope.** Says "I owned the pipeline rewrite" not "I drove
  transformational change." Will note what *didn't* work.
- **Lightly informal.** Comfortable with contractions and the occasional dry
  aside; not stiff, not jokey.

## What NOT to Do

- No buzzword stacking ("leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class").
- No inflated verbs ("spearheaded," "orchestrated") where "built" or "led"
  is true.
- No vague impact claims without a mechanism or number behind them.
- No artificial enthusiasm ("I'm thrilled to apply!!!").
- Don't open a cover letter with "I am passionate about..." — lead with what
  you built and why this role connects to it.

## What TO Do

- Lead with the concrete: the system, how it works, what it improved.
- Tie the role to your actual track record, not your feelings about it.
- Use precise technical terms in plain sentence structure.
- Be direct about motivation — "I want this role because X."
- Keep it tight: every sentence earns its place.
- Frame accomplishments as problems solved, not awards won.

## Cover Letter Guidelines

The cover letter should read like Alex explaining to a smart peer why this
specific role is a fit. Structure:

1. **Opening:** what the company/team is building and the one specific thread
   connecting it to Alex's real work (not "passion").
2. **Middle:** 2-3 concrete things he built or operated that are directly
   relevant, with enough technical detail to be credible. Narrative, not
   bullets.
3. **Close:** why the timing fits and a low-key, direct call to action.

Total length: 3-4 short paragraphs. No filler.

## Resume Guidelines

- Professional experience only — no interests or personal statements.
- Rewrite skill categories to emphasize what's relevant to the target role.
- Experience bullets should be specific and technical: what was built, with
  what, and the measurable outcome.
- Reorder sections to lead with the most relevant work for the target role.
- Never fabricate experience, tools, or metrics he doesn't have.
